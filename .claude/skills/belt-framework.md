# BELT Framework: The Durability Test

BELT predicts whether a product, feature, or habit will keep an audience or fizzle out. It was built for SaaS retention, but it works for anything that lives or dies on people coming back, including a daily webcomic. The framework tests four things, in order.

## The Four Questions

### B: Behavior
**Question: Are you building on an existing behavior, or trying to create a new one?**

Most things fail because they try to invent a behavior that doesn't exist. Changing your own habits is hard. Changing thousands of people's habits is nearly impossible.

**The test:** Can you describe what your readers already do today (without you) that you make better? If you can't, you have a behavior problem.

**Pass examples:**
- Uber: people already hailed cabs. Uber made it easier. Innovation on top of existing behavior.
- Startup Katt: people already scroll for a quick laugh and already read webcomics. A daily strip about startup life replaces a low-signal scroll with a higher-signal one that still feels light.

**Fail examples:**
- Asking readers to "remember to check the site" with nothing to attach that to.
- A feature that needs a brand-new ritual no one currently has (e.g. logging which strips they liked into a profile).

**Applying it to Startup Katt:** name the existing behavior the idea attaches to, specifically. "People already get comics emailed to them" is better than "people like comics." If an idea needs a new behavior, say so and flag the risk.

---

### E: Enduring Problem
**Question: Does this need go away once met, or does it keep coming back?**

**The test:** if a reader gets exactly what they want from you today, do they want you again tomorrow?

**Enduring (good):**
- "I want a small daily dose of humor about the work/startup life I'm living." Recurs every single day as long as people work.
- The relief of seeing your own absurd situation drawn by someone else. Never stops.

**Transient (bad):**
- "I want to read this one strip everyone linked to." Once read, done.
- A one-off topical joke about a news event nobody will search for in a month.

**Applying it to Startup Katt:** frame the need underneath the obvious one. The enduring problem isn't "I want to read a comic." It's "work is absurd and I want a daily two-second release that makes me feel seen." That recurs forever, which is exactly what a daily strip is built for.

---

### L: Lock-ins
**Question: What keeps readers coming back instead of drifting off, and how do you build that?**

Lock-ins beat features. A reader who loves one strip but has no reason to return is not retained.

**Types of lock-in to build for a webcomic:**

1. **Habit / streak:** "I read Startup Katt with my morning coffee." A daily cadence they slot into an existing routine.
2. **Subscription:** newsletter / RSS-to-email so the strip comes to them and they never have to remember the site.
3. **Archive investment:** the more of the back catalogue they have read, the more it costs them to walk away (binge history, in-jokes they now get).
4. **Identity / emotional attachment:** they relate to the character and quote the running gag (the cat is "Startup Katt" but everyone calls him "Startup Cat").
5. **Social:** they share strips, so their network expects them to be the one who posts the good ones.

The best lock-ins are psychological and habitual, not technical. If skipping a day feels like missing something, you win.

---

### T: Transient Distractions
**Question: Are you chasing temporary hits that won't drive long-term return visits?**

**The test:** for each idea, ask whether it still matters to the reader in six months. If not, it's a distraction.

**Distractions a webcomic should refuse to chase:**
- One-off viral gimmicks that spike traffic but build no habit.
- Heavy gamification (points, badges) that feels performative and off-brand.
- Feature/merch bloat before the daily reading habit even exists.
- Chasing every trend to the point the voice gets diluted.

The key insight: this is a daily ritual. Rituals die when they get complicated.

---

## Running BELT as a Quick Check

When sizing up any new idea (a feature, a format, a channel), run the four questions:

1. **B:** What existing behavior does this attach to? (If none: red flag.)
2. **E:** Will readers want this again next week? (If no: not worth building for retention.)
3. **L:** What would make it cost something to drift away? (If nothing: no staying power.)
4. **T:** What should this idea refuse to do? (If unclear: scope-creep risk.)

An idea that scores well on all four has a foundation for return visits. One that fails Behavior or Enduring Problem has a structural flaw. One that fails Lock-ins or Transient Distractions has a fixable strategy problem.
