# Reforge Growth Frameworks Skill

Use this knowledge when advising on SaaS growth strategy, retention, activation, growth loops, or growth models. Apply these frameworks when writing clarity maps, analyzing products, or building growth-related features.

---

## 1. Qualitative Growth Model

A qualitative growth model maps out how a product grows without needing exact numbers. It identifies the key loops and levers.

### Building a Qualitative Growth Model
1. **Identify your acquisition loops**: How do new users find the product?
2. **Map retention mechanics**: What keeps users coming back?
3. **Find monetization hooks**: How does usage convert to revenue?
4. **Connect the loops**: Show how retention feeds acquisition (more retained users = more referrals, more content, more network value).

The growth model is NOT a funnel. It is a system of interconnected loops.

---

## 2. Growth Loops (Not Funnels)

Growth loops replace the outdated "funnel" mental model. A loop is a closed system where the output of one cycle becomes the input of the next.

### Three Categories of Growth Loops

**1. Viral Loops**
- Personal: User invites others directly (e.g., Dropbox referral)
- Informational: User creates content that attracts new users (e.g., Pinterest pins in Google search)
- Financial: User gets incentive for bringing others (e.g., PayPal referral bonus)

**2. Content Loops**
- User-generated content (UGC): Users create content that gets indexed and attracts new users
- Company-generated content (CGC): Company creates SEO content that attracts users

**3. Paid Loops**
- Revenue from retained users funds acquisition spend, which brings new users who generate revenue

### Key Principles
- Every product has a dominant loop. Find yours and invest heavily.
- Loops compound. Funnels do not. That is why loops beat funnels.
- The quality of each cycle matters. A leaky loop (low retention) will never compound.
- Most sustainable businesses have 2-3 loops working together, but one is always dominant.

## 3. Retention: The Core of Growth

Retention is the CENTER of any growth model. Without retention, nothing else works. Retention increases everything: acquisition loops spin faster, monetization grows, virality compounds.

### The Bullseye Framework (Work Inside Out)

```
[Center]  Personas + Use Cases
          Retention Metric
          Visualize + Analyze
[Outer]   Strategies to Improve
```

Always work from the inside out. Start with understanding natural behavior, then build metrics, then visualize, then strategize.

### Step 1: Discover Natural Behavior (Use Cases + Personas)

Define use cases with five components:
1. **The Problem**: What is the problem in the user's own words?
2. **The Persona**: Who has this problem?
3. **The Why**: What is the core reason they choose your solution?
4. **The Alternative**: What would they do instead? (NOT direct competitors, but alternative solutions to the problem)
5. **The Frequency**: How often do they experience the problem?

**Nature vs. Nurture**: Nature = inherent behaviors and natural usage patterns. Nurture = manufactured or prompted behaviors (notifications, emails). You must align nurture to nature. Too much nurture = spam. Too little = users forget.

**Use Case Frequency Spectrum**:
- Daily-Monthly = **Habit Zone** (easier to build habits, stay top of mind: Slack, Instagram, Zoom)
- Monthly+ = **Forgettable Zone** (hard to stay top of mind: Airbnb, TurboTax, Carvana)
- If stuck in the forgettable zone, layer on higher-frequency use cases (e.g., Zillow: buying a home is yearly, but Zestimate checking is weekly)

**Common Mistakes**:
- Using too many use cases (you cannot build for everything)
- Not establishing anti-personas (who you are NOT targeting)
- Undifferentiated use cases (overlapping too much in frequency and behavior)

### Step 2: Choose a Retention Metric

Three components: **Frequency + Core Behavior + Who**

**Frequency**: Match the natural frequency of the problem. Validate with a histogram of last 28 days of use.
- Daily pattern: histogram congregates at 20+ days
- Weekly pattern: congregates around 3-7 days
- Monthly pattern: congregates at 1-2 days

**Core Behavior**: The simplest action that indicates value delivery. Validate by comparing retention curves for different action hypotheses. Pick the action that leads to the flattest, highest retention curve.

**Who**: Be specific about who is in the metric. Brand your metric clearly (e.g., WAR = Weekly Active Repinners, not just "WAU").

**Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Setting frequency too high (feels like spam, wrong direction)
- Setting frequency too low (users forget, wrong direction)
- Combining actions (creates gaming, team picks easiest action)
- Optimizing for revenue (revenue is output; usage is input. Usage drives value, value drives revenue)

**Examples**:
| Company | Use Case | Frequency | Core Behavior | Metric |
|---------|----------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Pinterest | Browsing | Weekly | Repin | WAR (Weekly Active Repinners) |
| Slack | Communication | Daily | Message | DAU (message exchanged) |
| Airbnb (Guest) | Travel | Yearly | Book night | YAG (Yearly Active Guest) |
| Zoom | Meeting | Weekly | Host meeting | WAH (Weekly Active Hosters) |
| Blue Bottle (Sub) | Subscription | Monthly | Receive sub | MAS (Monthly Active Subscriber) |
| Zillow | Monitor value | Monthly | View Zestimate | MAU (viewing Zestimate) |

### Step 3: Visualize Retention

Three essential visualizations:

**1. Lifecycle Bar Chart**: Shows distribution of new, retained, resurrected, and dormant users over time.

**2. Cohort Charts**: Four views:
- Retention Cohort Absolute: Raw numbers of users remaining per cohort per time period
- Retention Cohort Percentage: What % of users are left after X weeks?
- Retention Cohort Percentage Relative (Dormant): What % of starting cohort went dormant in each period?
- Retention Cohort Percentage Relative to Average: How does each cohort compare to the average?

**3. Retention Curve**: Plot the average cohort percentage over time as a line graph. This shows the shape and trend of retention.

### Step 4: Analyze Retention (Three Levels)

**Level 1: High-Level Diagnosis** (Do you have a bad, good, or great business?)

Four retention curve shapes:
- **Slope toward X-axis** = BAD. You will lose 100% of users over time. Growth flattens then declines.
- **Flat-ish** = OK. Slight slope toward zero but very slow. Engagement + resurrection optimization can make it flat.
- **Flat** = GOOD. You retain a certain % of every cohort forever. Foundation for sustainable growth.
- **Smile Curve** = GREAT. Curve decreases, flattens, then increases. Seen in network effect businesses (Slack, Airbnb).

Exception: Declining retention is OK for use cases with definitive starts and ends (Tinder, Zillow home buying, Pinterest project planning). The key is to layer on higher-frequency use cases.

**Determining Required Retention Rate** (from first principles, not benchmarks):
1. Project your retention rate out 5 years
2. Estimate 1-year value per user
3. Run TAM scenarios: Target Audience x 1-Year Value x 5-Year Retention Rate = Business Size
4. Vary % of market captured (100%, 50%, 25%, 10%) and retention rates to find what you need

Benchmarks by category (rough):
- B2C Social: ~60% (Facebook, Instagram)
- B2C Productivity: 35-40% (Evernote, Dropbox)
- B2C Subscription eComm: 25-40% (Birchbox, Dollar Shave Club)
- B2B Enterprise: 80-90%
- B2B Mid-Market: ~75%
- B2B VSB: 50-60%

**Level 2: Individual Cohort Trends** (Is retention improving or getting worse?)

Three questions:
1. Are cohorts shifting up or down over time? (Plot individual cohorts, not just average)
2. Where does the shift happen? (First time period = activation problem. Ongoing = engagement problem)
3. Is retention accelerating or decelerating? (Calculate avg % change per period for each cohort. Getting steeper = big problem)
4. Are there outlier cohorts? Use the "relative to average" view to find cohorts performing much better or worse. Investigate what was different.

**Level 3: Segmentation** (What are potential areas to improve?)

Eight common segmentation dimensions:
- Persona (role, job title, company size)
- Acquisition source (organic, paid, referral)
- Device/platform
- Feature usage
- Geography
- Time-based (day of week, season)
- Engagement depth
- Pricing tier

### Step 5: Improve Retention

**Retention is the OUTPUT. Do not try to improve it directly.** Break it into three inputs:

| Input | User Mindset | Business Goal |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Activation (Setup Moment) | "What do I do now?" | Get user to complete actions to setup the core value prop |
| Activation (Aha Moment) | "Why should I use this?" | Get user to experience core value prop ASAP |
| Activation (Habit Moment) | "When should I use this?" | Get user to establish a habit around core value prop |
| Engagement | "Why shouldn't I switch?" | Deepen engagement, increase investment so switching is harder |
| Resurrection | "Why should I give it another try?" | Change their already-formed negative opinion |

## 4. Activation: The Highest-Impact Lever

Activation is taking a user from signup to establishing a habit around your core value prop. It touches 100% of acquired users. Your first impression is your last impression.

### Why People Get Activation Wrong
1. **It is an afterthought**: Tacked on, not methodically designed
2. **Blind copying**: "If it worked for them, it will work for us" (wrong, different products/audiences)
3. **Prioritizing product goals over user goals**: Focusing on what the product wants, not what the user wants
4. **Cut off too early**: Defining activation as "setup complete" instead of "habit established"

### The Activation Flow (Work Backward from Success)

```
Success --> Habit --> Aha --> Setup --> Analyze
```

1. **Success**: Define the engaged, retained user state (the destination)
2. **Habit Moment**: Define when the user has established a habit
3. **Aha Moment**: Define when the user first experiences the core value prop
4. **Setup Moment**: Define the actions needed to set up for the aha moment
5. **Analyze**: Break down the flow piece by piece to generate experiment hypotheses

### User State Map

```
Signed Up --> Setup Moment --> Aha Moment --> Habit Moment --> Engaged
                                                                  |
                                                              Dormant
                                                                  |
                                                              Churned
```

### The Habit Moment

**Qualitative Definition**: What would the user say once they have established the habit? Write it in their words.
- Pinterest (browsing): "I find cool things on Pinterest every week."
- HubSpot Sales: "I now use HubSpot Sales for almost all of my sales emails."
- Zillow (buying): "I'm using Zillow as my primary tool to find and research homes."

**Quantitative Definition: XaY Format**
- X = number of times the user has done the core action
- a = action (from retention metric)
- Y = initial time period

Examples:
| Product | Habit Metric | Meaning |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Pinterest | 4r28 | Pinned/repinned 4 days out of first 28 days |
| Slack | 4d7 | Exchanged a message 4 days out of first 7 |
| HubSpot | 4d28 | Used core features 4 days out of first 28 |
| Airbnb | 2bY | Booked 2 times within first year |
| Zoom | 4d28 | Hosted 4 meetings within first 28 days |
| Zillow | 3w12 | Checked Zestimate 3 weeks out of first 12 |

**Finding Your Habit Moment Metric (3 Steps)**:

1. **Exploration**: Segment retention curves by XaY variations. Look for which segments retain dramatically better.

2. **Correlation**: Build a Habit Moment Matrix.
   - Rows: Core action count (1+, 2+, 3+, 4+, 5+)
   - Columns: Time periods (1 week, 2 weeks, 3 weeks, 4 weeks)
   - Each cell contains: Correlation, Sample Size, Positive Predictive Value (PPV), Negative Predictive Value (NPV)
   - Filter: Remove correlation < 0.3, remove small sample sizes, look for inflection points in correlation
   - Values to track:
     - Correlation: closer to 1 = better (how predictive of retention)
     - Sample Size: larger = better (statistical significance)
     - PPV: closer to 100% = better (% who DID reach this frequency and DID retain)
     - NPV: closer to 100% = better (% who did NOT reach this frequency and did NOT retain)

3. **Causation**: Correlation does not equal causation. Run experiments to verify. If you force users to the habit metric, does retention actually improve?

**Branding Your Metric**: Make it simple, focused, and measurable. Anyone should be able to communicate the goal in a single sentence.

### The Aha Moment

The moment the user first experiences the core value prop. It is NOT the same as activation (which goes all the way to habit). It is NOT "7 friends in 10 days" (that was Facebook's habit metric, often misattributed as the aha moment).

Define qualitatively (what does the user say?) then quantitatively (what metric correlates with reaching this moment and subsequently reaching the habit moment?).

### The Setup Moment

The actions users need to complete to set up for the aha moment. User mindset: "What do I do now?" Goal: Collect the minimum information needed to deliver the core value prop. Reduce friction here as much as possible.

### The Four Activation Fits

Four things that must align for activation to work:
1. **Audience-Message Fit**: Does the marketing message match what the target audience cares about?
2. **Message-Activation Fit**: Does the activation experience deliver on the marketing promise?
3. **Activation-Product Fit**: Does the activation experience lead to the core product value?
4. **Product-Channel Fit**: Does the product work well with the acquisition channel?

## 5. Applying These Frameworks

### Customer Retention Canvas

Use this template for any product analysis:

| Row | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| Use Case | Name of the use case |
| Problem | User's problem in their words |
| Persona | Who has this problem |
| Why | Core reason they choose your solution |
| Alternative | What they would do instead (not competitors) |
| Frequency | How often they experience the problem |
| Retention Metric | Frequency + Core Behavior + Who |
| Setup Moment | Actions to set up core value prop |
| Setup Metric | Measurable signal of setup completion |
| Aha Moment | First experience of core value prop |
| Aha Metric | Measurable signal of aha |
| Habit Moment | Established habit around core value prop |
| Habit Metric | XaY metric |
| Engagement Metric | Depth of ongoing usage (e.g., DAU Power/DAU) |

### When Writing About SaaS Products (Clarity Maps, Articles, etc.)

Always consider:
- What is the natural frequency of their core use case?
- Where are they on the frequency spectrum (habit zone vs. forgettable zone)?
- What is their likely retention curve shape?
- Have they correctly identified their core action, or are they tracking vanity metrics?
- Are they optimizing for revenue (output) instead of usage (input)?
- Is their activation cutting off too early (setup-only, no habit formation)?
- Do they have anti-personas defined, or are they trying to be everything to everyone?
